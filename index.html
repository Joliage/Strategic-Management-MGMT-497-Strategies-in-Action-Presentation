LO 5.1 · Objectives

Objectives come before strategies

Long-term objectives are the results expected from pursuing certain strategies; strategies are the actions taken to reach them.

04 / 54

What they are

Without long-term objectives, an organization drifts toward an unknown end or chases short-term fads and strays from its mission. The time frame for objectives and strategies should match — usually 2 to 5 years.

Common ways objectives are stated

Two kinds of objectives

Financial vs. strategic objectives

Both are common — and they often trade off against each other, forcing crucial choices.

07 / 54

💰 Financial objectives

  • Growth in revenues & earnings
  • Higher dividends & larger profit margins
  • Greater return on investment, higher EPS
  • Rising stock price & improved cash flow

🎯 Strategic objectives

  • Larger market share
  • Quicker on-time delivery than rivals
  • Lower costs & higher quality than rivals
  • Wider geographic coverage & tech leadership
  • New products to market ahead of rivals
The trade-off Raising prices to hit short-term financial targets can jeopardize long-term market share. Amazon operated for decades without profits, chasing market share instead — the best way to sustain advantage is to relentlessly pursue strategic objectives.

LO 5.3 · Integration

Vertical & horizontal integration

Forward + backward integration = vertical integration (control of distributors & suppliers). Horizontal integration = control of competitors.

13 / 54
⬆️

Forward

Move closer to the customer — own distributors or retailers.

⬇️

Backward

Move up the supply chain — own your suppliers.

↔️

Horizontal

Move sideways — own or control your competitors.

Coke caveat If Coke vertically integrated — buying sugar farms or opening its own stores — both would be excessively costly and divert it from what it does best: producing beverages. Joint ventures and alliances are usually the smarter alternative.
Integration · 1 of 3

Forward integration

Gaining ownership or control over distributors / retailers — cutting out the middleman. Franchising is an effective way to implement it.

14 / 54

Especially effective when…

  • Present distributors are expensive, unreliable, or can't meet needs
  • Quality distributors are scarce (a rival could lock an exclusive)
  • The industry is growing and expected to keep growing
  • The firm has the capital & people to run distribution
  • Stable production is especially valuable
  • Distributors have high profit margins worth capturing

Real example

Nike opens outlet stores and pushes direct-to-consumer sales; Nike.com revenue jumped 50%, aiming for $16B in direct sales.

Coke Assignment 10

Buy all the Coke bottlers it does not already own — moving Coke closer to consumers.

Integration · 2 of 3

Backward integration

Seeking ownership or control of a firm's suppliers — best when suppliers are unreliable, costly, or can't keep up.

15 / 54

Especially effective when…

  • Present suppliers are expensive, unreliable, or can't meet needs
  • Suppliers are few while competitors are many
  • The industry is growing rapidly
  • The firm has capital & people to run supply
  • Stable raw-material prices are critically important
  • Suppliers enjoy high profit margins
  • Resources may be needed quickly

Real example

Starbucks bought its first coffee farm — a 600-acre property in Costa Rica — to develop varieties and fight coffee rust.

Coke Assignment 10

Purchase three of its sugar cane & sugar beet suppliers to control its sweetener supply.

Integration · 3 of 3

Horizontal integration

Gaining ownership or control over competitors — arguably the most common growth strategy. Often meets antitrust challenges.

16 / 54

Especially effective when…

  • The firm can gain monopolistic strength without a federal challenge
  • It competes in a growing industry
  • Greater economies of scale are a major advantage
  • It has the capital & talent to manage a bigger organization
  • Competitors are faltering and can be bought at a discount
  • It wants to enter a new geographic market quickly

Real example

Walgreens acquired ~2,000 Rite Aid stores; Cineworld bought Regal for $3.6B to become the world's #2 cinema operator.

Coke Assignment 10

Take over Pepsi — absorbing its single largest competitor.

LO 5.4 · Intensive

Intensive strategies: stick to what you do best

These require intensive effort to improve a firm's position with existing products. Unlike integration or diversification, they keep the firm near its core.

17 / 54
1

Market penetration

Redouble your effort — sell more of present products in present markets.

2

Market development

Take what you do best "on the road" — new geographic areas.

3

Product development

Improve on what you do best — better or new products.

Intensive · 1 of 3

Market penetration

Increase market share for present products in present markets through greater marketing — more salespeople, advertising, promotions, and publicity.

18 / 54

Especially effective when…

  • Current markets are not saturated
  • Present customers' usage rate could rise significantly
  • Competitors' market shares fall while industry sales rise
  • Sales correlate highly with marketing spend
  • Greater economies of scale are a major advantage

Real example

Verizon spent millions on its "Humanability" campaign to win share in present markets.

Coke case link

A global ad & promotion blitz to shift perception of Coca-Cola from "unhealthy" to "treat yourself" — selling more in existing markets.

Intensive · 2 of 3

Market development

Introducing present products into new geographic areas. GDP is a guide for where to expand — but matching consumption habits matters even more.

19 / 54

Especially effective when…

  • New, reliable, inexpensive, quality channels are available
  • The firm is successful at what it does
  • New untapped or unsaturated markets exist
  • It has the capital & people for expanded operations
  • It has excess production capacity
  • Its industry is becoming global in scope
  • Consumption habits are similar in the new areas

Real example

Tesla began making and selling cars in China; Dollar General adds 1,000 stores a year in new rural locations.

Coke case link

Build a manufacturing plant in Africa to serve markets not yet saturated — global expansion is Coke's #1 future strategy.

Intensive · 3 of 3

Product development

Increasing sales by improving or modifying present products — usually with heavy R&D spending. A firm doesn't stray far from what it does best.

20 / 54

Especially effective when…

  • Products are in the maturity stage of the life cycle
  • The industry has rapid technological change
  • Competitors offer better-quality products at similar prices
  • The firm competes in a high-growth industry
  • It has especially strong R&D capabilities

Real example

Kellogg bought RXBAR to shift away from sugary cereals; Ford moved a third of its R&D budget to electric.

Coke Assignment 10

Introduce new healthy alternative beverages — exactly the "new healthy drinks" Coke's case calls for.

LO 5.5 · Diversification

Related vs. unrelated diversification

Businesses are "related" when value chains have competitively valuable cross-business fit; "unrelated" when they don't. Diversification is on the retreat — "management couldn't manage the beast."

22 / 54

Why related diversification appeals

  • Transfer expertise & technology between businesses
  • Combine related activities to lower costs
  • Exploit a common, well-known brand name
  • Cross-business collaboration builds capabilities

The bar diversification must clear

It must add more shareholder value than investors could create themselves by buying stock across firms. Any new industry must be attractive enough to yield consistently high returns and real synergies. Many strategists say firms should "stick to the knitting."

Diversification · 1 of 2

Related diversification

Adding new but related products that share a strategic fit with the firm's existing value chain.

23 / 54

Especially effective when…

  • The firm competes in a no-growth or slow-growth industry
  • Related products would lift sales of current products
  • Related products can be offered at competitive prices
  • Related products have seasonal sales that offset existing peaks & valleys
  • The firm has a strong management team

Real example

Disney acquired 21st Century Fox's film & TV studios — related entertainment assets including FX, Nat Geo and Hulu.

Coke Assignment 10

Introduce tea-based beverages — a new product line closely related to Coke's drinks business.

Diversification · 2 of 2

Unrelated diversification

Building a portfolio of businesses that can each deliver strong financial performance — rather than chasing strategic fit between them.

24 / 54

Especially effective when…

  • Existing markets for present products are saturated
  • The firm is in a highly competitive or no-growth industry
  • Present distribution channels can carry new products to current customers
  • New products have countercyclical sales patterns
  • The firm has the capital & managerial talent for a new industry

Real example

CVS acquired insurer Aetna; Amazon entered the $412B pharmacy business — both far from their original core.

Coke Assignment 10

Sell branded stationery products — an unrelated line with no fit to beverages.

LO 5.6 · Defensive

Defensive strategies

When a firm is struggling, it can regroup, sell a part, or sell everything. Retrenchment is the broad term that can include the other two.

25 / 54
🔧

Retrenchment

Regroup through cost & asset reduction — a turnaround.

✂️

Divestiture

Sell a division or part of the organization.

🏷️

Liquidation

Sell all assets, in parts, for their tangible worth.

Bankruptcy Chapter 11 lets a firm retrench, reorganize and come back. Chapter 7 is a liquidation procedure used when there's no hope of operating successfully.
Defensive · 1 of 3

Retrenchment (turnaround)

Regrouping through cost and asset reduction to reverse declining sales and profits — selling land, pruning product lines, closing plants, cutting staff.

26 / 54

Especially effective when…

  • The firm is plagued by inefficiency, low profits, poor morale & stockholder pressure
  • It has failed to use strengths and opportunities or fix weaknesses over time
  • It has grown so large, so fast that major reorganization is needed

Real example

Eli Lilly cut 8% of its workforce and redirected savings into R&D; GoPro laid off a fifth of staff and exited drones.

Coke Assignment 10

Close down Coca-Cola operations in Asia to cut costs and regroup around stronger regions.

Defensive · 2 of 3

Divestiture

Selling a division or part of an organization — often to raise capital, refocus on the core, and become less diversified.

27 / 54

Especially effective when…

  • Retrenchment failed to deliver needed improvements
  • A division causes the firm's overall poor performance
  • A division is a misfit — different markets, customers, or values
  • A large amount of cash is needed quickly
  • Government antitrust action threatens the firm

Real example

Nestlé divested its U.S. chocolate business to Ferrero for $2.8B to refocus on its core.

Coke Assignment 10

Sell Dasani — shedding the bottled-water division to raise capital and refocus.

Defensive · 3 of 3

Liquidation

Selling all of a company's assets, in parts, for their tangible worth — a recognition of defeat, and an emotionally difficult strategy.

28 / 54

Especially effective when…

  • Both retrenchment and divestiture were tried and failed
  • Stockholders can minimize their losses by selling the firm's assets

It can be better to cease operating than to keep losing large sums of money.

Real example

Toys "R" Us liquidated 885 U.S. stores (~33,000 jobs); Ringling Bros. closed after 146 years.

Coke Assignment 10

Sell Coke's secret formula / recipe — auctioning off the company's single most valuable asset.

LO 5.7 · Value chain

Value chain analysis (VCA)

VCA determines the value — price minus cost — of every activity that goes into producing and marketing a product, from raw materials to customer service.

29 / 54

Why it matters

Value activities are hard for rivals to copy, so they're a source of sustainable advantage. Just cutting price or hiring a celebrity is easily imitated. A firm should know not only its own value chain, but those of its industry, competitors, suppliers and distributors.

Two paths to value

To beat rivals, create value that exceeds theirs in one of two ways:

① Operate at the lowest cost② Command a premium price

Rolex creates value through prestige; Timex through price & utility — both win in the same industry.

LO 5.7 · Benchmarking

Benchmarking — wisely

Examining value-chain activities across an industry to find "best practices," then duplicating or improving on them.

32 / 54

How it helps

It identifies activities where rivals hold an advantage in cost, service, reputation, or operation — and lets a firm act to close the gap. Sources include published reports, trade publications, suppliers, distributors, customers, and willing rivals.

⚠ Never benchmark blindly

Five Guys should not copy McDonald's service-time, and McDonald's should not copy Five Guys' quality. Benchmarking the wrong variable can lead a firm away from what made it successful. Uniqueness still matters.

LO 5.8 · Porter

Porter's two generic strategies

Competitive advantage comes from two bases — cost leadership and differentiation. It's hard to compete on both at once. Each splits by market breadth into four types.

33 / 54

Rolex, Louis Vuitton

Cost leadership

Standardized products at the lowest per-unit cost for price-sensitive buyers. The firm reduces cost all along its value chain.

Differentiation

Products considered unique, for buyers who are relatively price-insensitive. The firm maximizes value all along its value chain.

Generic · cost

Cost leadership (Types 1 & 2)

Both target a large market to achieve economies of scale. Underprice rivals to gain share — but the advantage must be hard for rivals to copy.

34 / 54

The two types

  • Type 1 — broad low-cost: low prices to a wide range of customers (Walmart, Amazon)
  • Type 2 — focused low-cost: low prices to a small range of customers (Dollar General, Jiffy Lube, Little Caesars, Spirit Airlines)

Works best with many price-sensitive buyers, few ways to differentiate, and powerful buyers.

Risks

  • Competitors imitate, driving industry profits down
  • A technological breakthrough makes it ineffective
  • Buyers shift to other features besides price
  • Simply cutting retail prices is easily copied and erodes margins
Generic · differentiation

Differentiation (Types 3 & 4)

Less reliant on scale, so serving a small group can work if they'll pay a premium. Pursue only after careful study of buyers' needs.

35 / 54

The two types

  • Type 3 — broad differentiation: unique value to a wide audience (Monster Beverage, Apple, BMW)
  • Type 4 — focused differentiation: unique value to a narrow audience (Louis Vuitton, Rolex, Maserati)

Bases include superior service, design, performance, useful life, or ease of use. Apple differentiates through its own iOS.

Risks

  • Customers may not value the uniqueness enough to justify the price
  • Competitors may quickly copy the differentiating features

Find durable sources of uniqueness that can't be imitated quickly or cheaply.

LO 5.9 · Means

Build, borrow, or buy?

Under constant pressure to grow ~4%+ and raise dividends, firms choose among three ways to achieve their strategies.

37 / 54
🏗️

Build

Grow internally (organically) — hire, train, build, or find a blue ocean.

🤝

Borrow

Grow with others — joint ventures & strategic alliances.

💵

Buy

Grow by acquiring — mergers, acquisitions, LBOs.

Means · build

Build from within (organic growth)

New training, new hires, building your own stores — or a blue ocean strategy that targets a new market where no rival yet competes.

38 / 54

🌊 Blue ocean vs. red ocean

A blue ocean is uncontested market space; a red ocean is crowded, often competing on price. Kim & Mauborgne found 86% of new products are line extensions — only 14% target new markets.

Examples

Apple's PC and smartphone, plus Netflix, Southwest, eBay and Amazon, were all partly blue-ocean — customers didn't even know they wanted them. But blue oceans rarely last: rivals enter fast.

Means · borrow

Borrow — joint ventures & alliances

Two or more firms form a temporary partnership with shared equity to seize an opportunity too complex, costly, or risky to pursue alone.

39 / 54

Why borrow?

A firm borrows when it can't build the resources internally, or when merging is too costly/risky. JVs improve networking, globalize operations, and minimize risk.

Coke & Nestlé ran "Beverage Partners Worldwide" — a tea JV they later dissolved

Why JVs struggle

  • The daily managers weren't involved in forming it
  • It benefits the partners but not customers
  • It isn't supported equally by both partners
  • It starts to compete with one partner more than the other
Means · buy

Buy — mergers & acquisitions

A merger unites two firms of about equal size; an acquisition is a large firm buying a smaller one (or vice versa). Unwanted = a hostile takeover.

40 / 54

Why M&A often fails

  • Integration difficulties across both value chains
  • Too much new debt
  • Inability to achieve synergy
  • Too much diversification
  • Hard to merge different cultures
  • Reduced morale from layoffs & relocations

Also worth knowing

A leveraged buyout (LBO) uses borrowed funds to take a public firm private. Private-equity firms buy firms cheap to sell them later high.

Keurig Green Mountain acquired Dr Pepper Snapple in 2018 — aimed squarely at Coke

LO 5.10 · First mover

First-mover advantages & disadvantages

Benefits of entering a market or launching a product before rivals — like taking the high ground first. But moving first carries real risk.

42 / 54

Six first-mover benefits

  • Secure access to rare resources
  • Gain knowledge of critical success factors
  • Win market share & the best locations
  • Lock in long-term relationships
  • Build customer loyalty
  • Gain patent protection early

When to be a fast follower instead

Being a slow / late mover works when a rival's product is easy to copy, especially when initial costs are high. If technology advances quickly, fast followers can leapfrog with improved second-generation products — as seen in electric cars, where adoption is slowed by switching costs and charging infrastructure.

Apple was a struggling first mover in PCs, but a dominant first mover in iPods and iPhones.

LO 5.11 · Special cases

Nonprofit, government & small firms

Strategic planning is just as vital outside the for-profit corporation — sometimes more so.

43 / 54

Nonprofits

Differ from for-profits in just two ways:

  • They don't pay taxes
  • They have no shareholders for capital

They still have customers, suppliers, budgets & competitors that want to put them out of business.

Government agencies

Operate with less strategic autonomy — they usually can't diversify or merge, and politics narrows their choices. Strategic planning helps justify funding requests.

Small firms

From day one every firm has a strategy, even if it just evolves. Planning is often informal, but small firms that do it generally outperform those that don't.

Assignment 10 · Question 1

Strategy types vs. actual strategies

"Explain the difference between strategic types and actual strategies."

49 / 54

Strategy types — the general categories

Strategies are plans of action used to attain long-term goals. They come in broad types that describe the kind of move — and the level at which it's made:

  • Corporate — high-level direction & long-term wellbeing
  • Business — competitive advantage & market share
  • Functional — optimize each department's capabilities

In Chapter 5 terms, the "types" are the 11 generic strategies (forward integration, market penetration, and so on).

Actual strategies — the specific plan

An actual strategy is a clear, specific plan of action that defines exactly what an organization will do, over a defined period, to reach a specific goal.

Type Forward integration  →  Actual "Buy all Coke bottlers it doesn't already own"

The type is generic; the actual strategy is specific to the company. Be specific to the extent possible.

Wrap-up

The strategist's takeaway

54 / 54

What to remember

  • Set clear, quantitative objectives before choosing strategies
  • Eleven strategy types fall into four families — pick a focused few, not all
  • Intensive strategies keep a firm near its core; integration & diversification move it away
  • Use value chain analysis & benchmarking to find durable advantage
  • Compete on cost or on differentiation — rarely both — and grow by building, borrowing, or buying

A "strategic plan" is a "game plan"

Success depends on having a better plan than your rival's. As parity grows, the value of the plan grows exponentially — for Coca-Cola and for any firm.
